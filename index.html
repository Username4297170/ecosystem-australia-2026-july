The Shape, Spread, and Structure of Australia’s Fee-for-Service Life-Sciences Capability

Mapping the capabilities Australian life-sciences Providers offer on a fee-for-service basis: the specialist work that can be outsourced.

Authors  James Cooney and Nenad Sejic Dataset  Pipeline Bio Database Snapshot  July 2026 Scope  258 Providers · 5,944 fee-for-service capabilities

Abstract

This report explores the Australian life-sciences Provider landscape, drawn from the Pipeline Bio Database. As of July 2026, the Database holds 258 Providers publishing 5,944 fee-for-service capabilities, mapped across a structured taxonomy (Pipeline Bio’s Capability Taxonomy). The figures in this report reflect fee-for-service capabilities; the Platform is also expanding to list collaboration and licensing capabilities, which sit outside these counts.

Fee-for-service R&D capability in Australia spans the full research and development value chain, with particular depth in clinical delivery and pharmaceutical manufacturing in Victoria and New South Wales, and a Provider base that skews towards specialist organisations. This report maps that distribution across the eight core categories of the value chain and considers what it means for biotechs and Providers.

01Background

Mapping Australia’s capabilities

Biotech, medtech, pharmaceutical, and research teams routinely depend on external Providers for the specialist work that sits between an idea and an approved product. The challenge in Australia is not always whether a capability exists, but whether it can be found, evaluated, and engaged.

That difficulty has a structural cause: the country’s scientific-services landscape has never been mapped in a consistent, quantitative way. Austrade has published a useful high-level Life Sciences Industry Capability Report1, but it lacks the granularity a research team needs to pinpoint a specific capability for a project. Provider discovery therefore happens through informal networks, ad hoc searches, and word of mouth, leaving a sector where a team can struggle to identify a Provider it needs even when that Provider already exists.

The Pipeline Bio Platform addresses that gap with a Directory of Providers and by cataloguing capability against a structured Capability Taxonomy. This report uses that catalogue to ask three foundational questions: how much capability exists nationally and how it is distributed across scientific categories; where that capability physically sits across the States and Territories; and how it is organised across the Providers that hold it.

That matters beyond individual buyer–Provider interactions. A clearer map of Australian capability reduces friction for international companies evaluating Australia, improves the use of existing infrastructure, and shows the sector where national capability clusters or runs shallow. It may also help inform policy and targeted government support when considered alongside demand-side evidence.

02Methods

Data, measures, and scope

The data source for this report is the Pipeline Bio Provider Database2, captured as a snapshot in July 2026. At that point the Database held 258 Providers and 5,944 catalogued fee-for-service capabilities, each classified against a structured Capability Taxonomy. Data was collected by Pipeline Bio from publicly available information: Provider websites, brochures, and interviews, or entered into the Pipeline Bio Database by Providers themselves.

Pipeline Bio’s Capability Taxonomy is a four-level hierarchy that moves from broad areas of activity to increasingly specific ones. It is designed around how teams search for external R&D capabilities and how Providers present them, so some branches are subdivided more finely than others. Level 1 (L1) captures the major categories of the life-sciences value chain, such as Clinical, Preclinical, Regulatory Affairs, and Pharmaceutical Manufacturing, CMC & Testing; each is divided into Level 2 (L2), Level 3 (L3), and, where appropriate, Level 4 (L4) categories. This allows every capability to be classified consistently while preserving enough granularity to distinguish broad areas from highly specific technical work. In total the Taxonomy comprises 13 L1, 77 L2, 434 L3, and 541 L4 categories. The full structure can be explored as an interactive sunburst or table at www.pipelinebio.com/resources.3

Figure 1 reports the whole Database across all 13 L1 categories. The figures that follow focus on eight core categories of the scientific research and development value chain: Clinical; Pharmaceutical Manufacturing, CMC & Testing; Preclinical; Post-Approval, Market Access & Lifecycle; Regulatory Affairs; Data, Statistics & Software; Quality Systems, Compliance & Assurance; and Medical Device Capabilities. The other five categories (Supply Chain & Logistics; Facilities, Equipment & Engineering; Financial & RDTI; Legal & IP; Organisational & Corporate) cover professional, operational, and corporate capabilities that sit outside the scientific value chain, and so are not the subject of these figures in this report. The full Level 2 to Level 4 breakdown of all 13 categories is provided as interactive explorers in the supplementary figures (Figures S1–S13).

Note on capability types presented in this report. Every capability listed on the Platform is tagged as fee-for-service, collaboration, or licensing. The figures throughout this report reflect fee-for-service capabilities only.

Inclusion in the Database indicates that a capability has been identified and classified from available information; it does not assess the quality, suitability, regulatory status, capacity, or performance of that capability, nor does it constitute an endorsement of any particular Provider or capability by Pipeline Bio.

03Results

Australia’s capability data

Capability coverage

The Database holds 258 Australian Providers, between them publishing 5,944 fee-for-service capabilities that map across the Capability Taxonomy (Figure 1). 36.2% of all capabilities sit within the top two L1 categories, Clinical and Pharmaceutical Manufacturing, CMC & Testing, and 49.6% of Providers operate across three or more L1 categories.

The Clinical and Pharmaceutical Manufacturing, CMC & Testing categories comprise the most capabilities (1,200 and 954 respectively), followed by Preclinical (679), Post-Approval, Market Access & Lifecycle (425), Regulatory Affairs (378), Data, Statistics & Software (334), Quality Systems, Compliance & Assurance (308), and Medical Device Capabilities (245) (Figure 2.1). Together these eight core categories hold 4,523 capabilities, of which the two largest account for 47.6%.

Measured by the number of distinct Providers active in each category, Clinical has the widest Provider participation, with 108 Providers. Preclinical, Regulatory Affairs, and Pharmaceutical Manufacturing, CMC & Testing are also strongly represented, with 85, 83, and 82 Providers respectively (Figure 2.2). Quality Systems, Compliance & Assurance (69) and Post-Approval, Market Access & Lifecycle (68) follow, then Data, Statistics & Software (65) and Medical Device Capabilities (34).

Dividing each category’s capability count by the number of active Providers gives a capability-density ratio: the average number of capabilities held by each Provider in that category (Figure 2.3). Pharmaceutical Manufacturing, CMC & Testing is the densest category, at 11.63 capabilities per Provider, closely followed by Clinical at 11.11. Preclinical sits just above the eight-category mean of 7.3, while Medical Device Capabilities, Post-Approval, Market Access & Lifecycle, Data, Statistics & Software, Regulatory Affairs, and Quality Systems, Compliance & Assurance all sit below it.

Descending one level of the taxonomy splits each core category into its constituent L2 categories.

Within Clinical, the weight sits in Trial Delivery (415 capabilities) and Clinical Research Lab (345), with Trial Management (254) and Medical Writing and Communications (186) making up the rest. CMC Analytical Development & Testing (384) and Drug Product Manufacturing (250) are the largest blocks within Pharmaceutical Manufacturing, with the remaining categories tapering quickly (Figure 3). Preclinical is more evenly spread, the largest categories being Research-Grade Production & Reagents (166) and Molecular Biology (117). Quality Systems is the most compact, resolving into three L2 categories led by Quality Assurance and QMS (138). The full L2 to L4 composition of each category can be explored in the supplementary figures (Figures S1–S13).

At L3, Clinical & Research Data Management houses the most capabilities (100), followed by Drug Product Formulation Development (93), Regulatory Strategy Development & Roadmap (88), and Development & Scientific Advisory (86), with the remainder of the top thirty tapering to a cut-off at 37 (Figure 4.1). Together these thirty L3 categories account for 28.7% of all catalogued capabilities.

Counting capabilities that resolve to an L4 category, the most-served L4 category within the eight core categories is Clinical Data Management (43), followed by Regulatory Consulting & Advice (34), Planning & Support (24), and Study Report Writing and Compliance Auditing & Consulting (21 each), tapering to a cut-off at 10 (Figure 4.2). These thirty L4 categories account for 7.8% of all capabilities in the Database, and 20.6% of the 2,239 core capabilities catalogued to full L4 depth.

Geographic distribution

Among the 209 Providers with at least one core capability, Provider presence is concentrated in Victoria and New South Wales, with 110 and 88 Providers respectively. Queensland accounts for 53 Providers, South Australia 29, Western Australia 20, the ACT 10, Tasmania 9, and the Northern Territory 7 (Figure 5.1). Because Providers operating in more than one State or Territory are counted in each jurisdiction, the State and Territory counts sum to more than 209.

A similar pattern appears when core capabilities are counted rather than Providers. Victoria accounts for 2,241 State-level capability listings and New South Wales for 1,961, followed by Queensland (782), South Australia (535), Western Australia (277), Tasmania (215), the ACT (68), and the Northern Territory (66) (Figure 5.2). Because each capability is counted in every jurisdiction where its Provider operates, the eight jurisdictions together hold 6,145 State-level listings. Victoria and New South Wales account for 68% of these listings, compared with 61% of Provider listings in Figure 5.1.

Resolving the data into a State-by-category grid shows that Preclinical, Pharmaceutical Manufacturing, and Clinical capability is concentrated most heavily in Victoria and New South Wales (Figure 5.3). The largest individual cells are Clinical in Victoria, with 577 capabilities, and Clinical in New South Wales, with 501. Clinical has the greatest depth, with 1,593 State-level listings, and is the largest core category in Victoria, New South Wales, Queensland, and South Australia. Pharmaceutical Manufacturing, CMC & Testing is the largest category in Western Australia and Tasmania, while Post-Approval, Market Access & Lifecycle is the largest in the ACT and Northern Territory. The pattern also shows areas of narrower coverage. In Tasmania, 113 capabilities sit in Pharmaceutical Manufacturing, CMC & Testing, representing more than half of the State’s core total. Medical Device Capabilities is the smallest core category, with 285 State-level listings. Most of this activity is recorded in Victoria (176) and New South Wales (75), while Western Australia, the ACT, and the Northern Territory record no Medical Device Capability listings in the current dataset.

Provider structure

Crossing Company Category with core capability category shows where each type of Provider delivers (Figure 6). Company Category is a Provider’s description (for example, CRO or CDMO) and is distinct from the capability categories of the taxonomy; a Provider may carry more than one. CROs are the dominant presence in Clinical (826 capabilities) and carry sizeable footprints in Preclinical (236), Data, Statistics & Software (211), Post-Approval (208), and Regulatory Affairs (180); CDMOs concentrate in Pharmaceutical Manufacturing, CMC & Testing (576), with a secondary presence in Medical Device (95) and Preclinical (77); and Tools and reagents Providers lead Preclinical (261) and Pharmaceutical Manufacturing (139). Consultancies are the most evenly distributed type, with high counts in Clinical (251), Post-Approval (230), Quality Systems (198), Regulatory Affairs (193), Pharmaceutical Manufacturing (146), and Data (100). Because a Provider counts in every Category it carries, capabilities offered by multi-Category Providers appear under more than one row.

Provider breadth varies across the core value chain (Figure 7). Of the 209 Providers with at least one core L1 capability, 115 Providers, or 55%, are active in one or two core categories. At the other end of the spectrum, 62 Providers, or 30%, span four or more core categories, a group of broader Providers able to support multiple parts of the development pathway. A further 32 Providers, or 15%, sit between these two groups, with capabilities across exactly three core categories.

When the value chain is grouped into four R&D stages, Preclinical, Clinical, Manufacturing, and Post-Market, most Providers operate across one or two stages: 82 Providers, or 39%, are active in a single stage, while 60, or 29%, span two (Figure 8). A smaller group extends across three stages (23 Providers, 11%), and 22 Providers, also 11%, operate end-to-end across all four. A Provider is counted as spanning a stage where its capability footprint includes at least one category assigned to that stage. The remaining 22 Providers are core-capable but do not fall into any of the four defined stages, because their activity sits entirely in cross-cutting categories such as Regulatory Affairs, Data, Statistics & Software, and Quality Systems, which support the development pipeline rather than representing a discrete stage within it.

04Discussion

Australia’s capability profile has a clear and consistent shape: two long arms of clinical and manufacturing capability, holding 47.6% of all core capability, centring in Victoria and New South Wales, and a broad but shallower base across everything that surrounds them. Yet the ecosystem is not narrow: 49.6% of Providers span three or more L1 categories (measured across all 13 L1 categories, not the eight core categories alone), so capability is networked rather than siloed. A question the data raises is whether the shorter axes reflect deliberate, demand-led prioritisation or genuine gaps that constrain how much end-to-end work can be performed in Australia.

With 34 active Providers, Medical Device Capabilities is the category where teams outsourcing R&D may have the least choice and where single-source dependency risk is highest. It is also the most geographically concentrated, and physical production is where the state-level gaps sit: both medical-device and pharmaceutical manufacturing peak in Victoria and New South Wales, with the Australian Capital Territory and Northern Territory recording none of either and Western Australia none of medical devices, whereas clinical, regulatory, and data capability are present in every State. This may be an expected shape for capability that leans on capital-intensive facilities.

The Provider base is specialist-leaning with a little over half of core-capable Providers working in one or two core categories, and 22 span all four R&D stages end-to-end. For most outsourcing, that means assembling several focused Providers rather than relying on one broad Provider.

Capability density shows that Pharmaceutical Manufacturing, CMC & Testing and Clinical have the highest number of catalogued capabilities per active Provider in the current Database, while Regulatory Affairs and Quality Systems, Compliance & Assurance have the lowest. This should be read as a catalogue-structure measure rather than a direct measure of relative market depth, buyer choice, or category strength. Assessing capabilities within each L1 category against those of other nations, or against a defined set of all plausible capabilities within that category, would be informative analyses to conduct, and will be the focus of future reports.

As a supply-side map, the dataset counts what Providers offer, not what biotech and medtech teams are looking for, and that distinction matters most for the thinner categories. A thin category may mark an undersupplied area, where demand exists but local capability does not. This is the case that should most concern policymakers, because unmet domestic demand tends to be met offshore. Alternatively, it may reflect a category where demand has been too thin to sustain more Providers, in which case supply-side intervention risks funding capacity that goes unused. Read alongside demand-side evidence, this data can help direct efforts and programs towards closing genuine gaps and reinforcing existing strengths. A future study will bring demand-side data into the picture so that supply gaps can be separated from demand gaps. This information may support efforts to strengthen Australia’s sovereign capability and international competitiveness.

Bringing the Australian ecosystem into a single structured Directory of Providers and Capability Taxonomy is what makes it discoverable and comparable, so that capability is weighed on its merits rather than missed for want of visibility.
